Back
01
T-Bar Bent-Over Row ›
Mid-back thickness · Rhomboids · Rear delts
3 × 12–16
Cues
  • Hip hinge ~45°; chest up, back flat throughout
  • Pull handle to lower sternum; elbows stay close
  • Squeeze shoulder blades at the top — hold 1 sec
  • Full stretch at bottom; don't round upper back under load
Pro Tip
This is your primary back thickness builder. The hip-hinge position mirrors a takedown stance. Load progressively — add 2.5 kg when you can hit the top of the rep range easily.
02
Lat Pulldown ›
Lat width · Lower lats · Upper-back sweep
3 × 12–16
Cues
  • Lean back 15°; initiate by depressing scapula first
  • Pull bar to upper sternum — not behind the neck
  • Full stretch at top every rep; let shoulder blades rise
  • Thumbless grip removes forearm interference
Pro Tip
Think "pull your elbows to your pockets" not "pull the bar down." This mental cue shifts the load from biceps to lats instantly.
03
Seated Row ›
Mid-back · Rhomboids · Constant cable tension
3 × 14–20
Cues
  • Slight forward lean at start for full lat stretch
  • Pull to upper abdomen; elbows hug torso
  • Squeeze blades at peak; slow return to full stretch
Pro Tip
Higher rep range (14–20) here creates metabolic stress for back density. The constant cable tension is a different stimulus than free-weight rows.
04
Pullover ›
Lats · Serratus · Long head triceps · Rib cage expansion
2 × Failure
Cues
  • Lie across bench; hips low to maximise lat stretch
  • Arms straight (soft elbow bend); arc from overhead to hips
  • Feel the lat stretch at full extension — don't rush it
  • Go to true muscular failure; last set leave nothing behind
Pro Tip
Pullovers target the lats through a unique arc that rows and pulldowns can't replicate. They also develop the serratus anterior — visible as the "fingers" at the side of the chest on lean athletes.
Biceps
05
Preacher Curl ›
Short head · Zero cheating · Bicep width
3 × 12–16
Cues
  • Chest flat against pad; upper arms fully supported
  • Full extension at the bottom — don't cut the range
  • Slow curl; hard squeeze at top; use EZ-bar for wrist comfort
Pro Tip
The pad eliminates momentum entirely. When you can't cheat, every rep is pure muscle work — which is exactly why this is the most effective curl variation for growth.
06
Dumbbell Curl ›
Full bicep · Supination emphasis · Pump finisher
3 × Failure
Cues
  • Alternate or simultaneous — keep elbows pinned to sides
  • Supinate (twist pinky out) during the curl for peak contraction
  • Go to failure; last set should be difficult by rep 10+
  • No swinging — if you're swinging, the set is over
Pro Tip
Going to failure here after preacher curls = double the mechanical + metabolic stimulus. This combination is a classic high-volume bicep protocol.
Forearms
07
Reverse Grip Curls ›
Brachioradialis · Forearm extensors · Brachialis
3 × 12
Cues
  • Overhand (pronated) grip; elbows fixed at sides
  • Full ROM: complete extension to full contraction
  • Lighter than regular curls — control the weight
Pro Tip
Builds the brachialis which pushes the bicep up, creating arm thickness. Also directly develops grip endurance critical for pulling sports.
08
Reverse Wrist Curls ›
Forearm extensors (top of forearm)
3 × Failure
Cues
  • Forearms on thighs, backs of hands facing up
  • Extend wrists fully upward; use very light weight
  • Extensors are much weaker than flexors — respect the weight
Pro Tip
Training extensors alongside flexors creates balance and protects against tennis elbow — especially important with the high forearm volume in this program.
09
Bar Forearm Pushdown ›
Forearm extensors · Wrist stability
2 × 15
Cues
  • Cable machine, bar attachment at chest height; overhand grip
  • Push bar down by extending wrists; elbows fixed
  • Controlled return to full wrist flexion
Pro Tip
This is a relatively rare forearm exercise — the pushdown mechanic isolates the extensor compartment in a way no other exercise does. Great for complete forearm development.
10
Reverse Wrist Curls (2nd Set) ›
Extensor burnout
3 × 12
Cues
  • Same as above; slightly lighter since pre-fatigued
  • Focus on full range and peak contraction
Note
Second hit on extensors for full development. Forearms are high-frequency muscles — they recover quickly and respond well to this volume.
11
Wrist Curls ›
Forearm flexors · Grip strength
3 × 12
Cues
  • Sit; forearms on thighs, palms up, wrists over knees
  • Let bar roll to fingertips on descent — maximises ROM
  • Curl wrists up as high as possible; hold briefly
Pro Tip
The "roll to fingertips" on the way down doubles the range of motion compared to most people's technique. This is where most of the growth stimulus lives.

01
Incline DB Press — Heavy ›
Upper chest · Anterior delt · Strength focus
3 × 6–12
Cues
  • 30–45° bench; pinch shoulder blades before unracking
  • Lower DBs to outer chest; elbows ~75° from torso
  • Drive through palms; rotate slightly inward at top
  • Heaviest set of the workout — go here fresh
Pro Tip
This is your strength set — aim for 6-8 reps with a weight that's genuinely challenging. The heavy incline DB sets the upper-chest foundation that everything else builds on.
02
Incline Bench — Slow (40–50 kg) ›
Upper chest · Time-under-tension · Hypertrophy focus
16–20 reps
Cues
  • Moderate weight; 3-sec eccentric, 1-sec pause at bottom
  • Touch chest; full extension at top
  • The slow tempo is intentional — fight every rep
Why This Works
Back-to-back incline (heavy then high-rep slow) creates both mechanical tension AND metabolic stress on the upper pec. Two different growth signals = more complete stimulus.
03
Pec Deck Fly ›
Chest isolation · Full stretch position · Constant tension
3 × 18
Cues
  • Sit tall; forearms on pads or hands on handles
  • Bring pads together with a "hugging" motion; hold 1 sec
  • Control the return fully — that stretch is the stimulus
  • 18 reps: go moderate weight, full range priority
Pro Tip
Pec deck isolates the chest better than almost anything because there's no tricep involvement. The stretch at the open position is where most pec damage (and growth) occurs. Don't rush through it.
04
Flat Bench Press ›
Mid/overall chest · Horizontal push strength
2 × 6–12
Cues
  • Natural arch; feet drive into floor; leg drive = stability
  • Touch bar to lower sternum; elbows 45–60° to torso
  • "Pull the bar apart" to activate lats and protect shoulders
Note
Only 2 sets here because you've already pre-fatigued the chest with 6 incline/fly sets. These 2 sets of flat bench will feel harder than usual — that's the point.
05
Decline DB Press ›
Lower chest · Sternal fibres · Finishing pump
3 × 16
Cues
  • Decline bench; DBs at chest level, elbows wide
  • Press up and slightly inward; squeeze hard at top
  • 16 reps = moderate weight, pump focus
Pro Tip
Lower chest fibres only activate fully when pressing from a decline angle. Most people skip decline work and end up with underdeveloped lower pecs. This set fills that gap.
Triceps
06
Triceps Pushdown ›
Lateral + medial head · Lockout strength
3 × Failure
Cues
  • Rope or bar; slight forward hinge; elbows pinned to sides
  • Split rope at bottom for full lateral-head squeeze
  • Let rope rise to chin level for complete stretch
Pro Tip
Going to failure after 5 chest exercises means your triceps are already partially fatigued from pressing. You'll reach failure faster — that's expected. Don't adjust the weight up.
07
Cable Overhead Triceps Extension ›
Long head (largest head) · Peak stretch position
3 × Failure
Cues
  • Face away from cable; rope overhead, arms bent
  • Extend arms forward fully; hold squeeze at full extension
  • Return slowly — the stretch is the money position
Pro Tip
The overhead position is the only way to fully stretch the long head of the triceps — the largest of the three heads. Pairing this with pushdown (which hits the other heads) = complete tricep stimulus.

Shoulders
01
DB Press ›
Anterior + medial delt · Overhead strength base
3 × 8–14
Cues
  • Seated or standing; DBs at ear level, palms forward
  • Press up and slightly inward; full extension overhead
  • Control descent — don't drop below ear level
  • 8–14 range: heavier sets early, lighter as fatigue builds
Pro Tip
DB press allows each arm to move independently, fixing strength imbalances. If one arm is noticeably weaker, always start with the weaker side and match reps.
02
Cable Lateral Raise ›
Medial delt · Width builder · Constant tension
3 × 16–20
Cues
  • Cable at ankle height; lean slightly away from stack
  • Lead with elbow; tilt pinky up slightly at shoulder height
  • Controlled descent — 2 sec down minimum
  • 16–20 reps: this is a pump exercise, not a strength exercise
Cable vs Dumbbell
Cable provides constant tension through the full range; dumbbells drop to near-zero at the bottom. Cable lateral raises are objectively more effective for medial delt isolation — good choice here.
03
Reverse Pec Deck — Slow ›
Posterior delt · Rhomboids · Shoulder health
3 × Failure
Cues
  • Face the pec deck; arms forward, slight elbow bend
  • Drive elbows back and out in a wide arc
  • "Slow" is key — 3-sec eccentric; don't use traps to assist
  • Hold 1 sec at rear peak; feel the rear delt squeeze
Critical
Rear delts are almost universally underdeveloped because they're invisible in the mirror. They're essential for shoulder health, posture, and the 3D "capped" shoulder look. The slow tempo doubles the stimulus here.
04
Cable Shrugs ›
Upper traps · Neck-shoulder tie-in
3 × 12
Cues
  • Cable at low position; straight arms; shrug straight up
  • Hold at peak for 2 sec — don't roll the shoulders forward
  • Controlled descent; full range
Pro Tip
Cable shrugs keep constant tension vs barbell which loses it at the bottom. The upward shrug path (not forward roll) keeps the movement in the trap fibres, not the rotator cuff.

Legs
01
Heavy Squats ›
Quads · Glutes · Core stability · Primary strength movement
3 × 8–14
Cues
  • Bar on mid-trap shelf; brace core hard before every rep
  • Break at hips and knees simultaneously; descend to parallel+
  • Knees track over toes; drive through full foot
  • Chest stays tall; don't good-morning it up
  • Build to bodyweight squat by end of 3 months
Pro Tip
The squat is the king of lower-body exercises. Every rep strengthens your quads, glutes, core, and stabiliser muscles simultaneously. Track your squat numbers weekly — consistent progress here pays dividends everywhere.
02
Leg Extension ›
Quad isolation · VMO · Terminal extension
3 × 20
Cues
  • Align knee joint with machine's cam pivot point
  • Extend fully and squeeze the VMO at the top
  • 3-sec eccentric every rep; toes slightly inward for inner quad
  • 20 reps = moderate weight; metabolic pump focus
Pro Tip
20-rep leg extensions after heavy squats create brutal quad pump and metabolic stress — a different stimulus than the mechanical tension of the squat. Together, they're comprehensive.
03
Leg Curl ›
Hamstring isolation · Knee health · Posterior chain balance
3 × 20
Cues
  • Hips flat on pad; curl fully until heels near glutes
  • Hold peak contraction 1 sec; 3-sec eccentric
  • Pointed toes increases peak hamstring contraction
Why 20 Reps
Hamstrings have a high proportion of slow-twitch fibres and respond extremely well to high reps. 20-rep leg curls after 20-rep extensions = complete quad/ham balance, protecting the knee joint.

Back — Different Angles from Monday
01
Reverse Grip Bent-Over Row ›
Lower lats · Bicep involvement · Different pull angle
3 × 12–16
Cues
  • Underhand (supinated) grip; hip hinge same as T-bar row
  • Elbows stay closer to torso than overhand row
  • Pull to lower abdomen; supinated grip emphasises lower lats
Why Different from Monday
Monday's T-bar row used an overhand/neutral grip. Today's reverse grip shifts the load to lower lats and involves the biceps more heavily — a complementary stimulus for complete back development.
02
Lat Pulldown ›
Lat width — use different grip than Monday
3 × 12–16
Grip Rotation
  • Monday: V-bar or close neutral grip
  • Friday: Wide overhand OR underhand supinated
  • Different grips recruit different lat fibre regions
Pro Tip
Rotating grip weekly prevents adaptation and ensures you're developing width, thickness, and the full lat from top to bottom. Varying the stimulus is a core principle of the 2× frequency approach.
03
Seated Row ›
Mid-back · Second weekly hit with constant tension
3 × 14–20
Cues
  • Same execution as Monday; vary the attachment if available
  • Wide grip = more upper-back; close V-bar = more lower lat
Note
Second weekly hit on the same movement pattern builds strength faster than single weekly exposure. Consistency on this movement will show visible back thickness in 6–8 weeks.

Cues

  • Full stretch at overhead position — the key stimulus
Note
Pullover is kept both days because it's the only exercise that works the lats through a pure overhead stretch arc — a movement pattern nothing else replicates.

Hammer Curl ›
Brachialis · Brachioradialis · Arm thickness
3 × Failure
Cues
  • Neutral grip (thumbs up); elbows fixed at sides
  • Curl in a vertical plane; wrists stay neutral throughout

Why Different from Monday's Curls
Monday focused on supinated curls (long head, peak). Friday's hammer curl focuses on the brachialis — the muscle under the bicep that pushes it up and creates arm thickness. Together they develop the complete arm.

Incline DB Press — Heavy ›
Upper chest · Try to beat Tuesday's weight or reps
3 × 6–12
Saturday Strategy
  • Compare with Tuesday's performance — match or beat it
  • If you hit the top of the rep range (12), add 2 kg next week

Why Repeat
Exact repetition of the same session on Saturday is intentional. Your neuromuscular system has 4 days to recover since Tuesday — you should be slightly stronger. Track this. Progressive overload across both sessions = faster growth.
